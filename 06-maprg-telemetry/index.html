A Browser’s Perspective

Surprises and insights from Firefox networking telemetry.

MAPRG · IETF 126, Vienna · 23 July 2026  ·  Oskar Mansfeld & Max Inden · Mozilla

A client-side vantage point, at scale

  • Firefox reports anonymized networking telemetry from ~200M monthly active users (Firefox Public Data Report).
  • The client side, across the globe: it complements server-side and active-probing datasets.
  • Congestion control, DNS timings, path characteristics, QUIC adoption, Happy Eyeballs v3, and more.
  • Collected and aggregated with Glean, and entirely public: anyone can query it.

We use it every day to reason about the stack. Here are a few surprises, and how you can use it too.

Who we measure: the whole population

  • Windows dominates (~85%: Win 11 ~48%, Win 10 ~31%); macOS ~8%, Linux ~6%.
  • A real tail of old and small machines: ~7% run a 32-bit OS, a quarter have only 2 CPU cores, ~11% just 4 GB of RAM.
  • This is not the developer’s laptop. Networking code runs on all of it.

On mobile, a third of traffic is cellular

  • On Android: ~61% wifi, ~29% cellular, the rest unreported.
  • Cellular paths bring higher and more variable RTT, more loss, and NAT rebinding: the hard cases cluster here.
  • Desktop has no equivalent split, most of it is wired or wifi.

HTTP splits roughly in thirds

  • Across all responses: HTTP/3 ~30%, HTTP/2 ~35%, HTTP/1.1 ~35%.
  • The surprise: HTTP/1.1 is far from dead, still as common as HTTP/2.
  • A client-side answer to “how much HTTP/3?”: ~30% of responses, in line with Cloudflare’s request share.
  • Within HTTP/3, a new metric tracks QUIC v1 vs v2: ~100% v1 today, v2 not yet negotiated at scale (http_3_quic_version, Nightly).

How connections find HTTP/3

  • Only ~42% of connections are offered HTTP/3 at all.
  • Of those, ~84% learn it only from Alt-Svc, so after a first non-h3 connection: a wasted round trip.
  • An HTTPS record would move that discovery into DNS, before connecting, but almost nobody publishes one.

What HTTPS records actually carry

Where records exist, most advertise h3 and about half carry address hints, but only ~16% carry ECH, the feature that most needs them.

These records arrive over DNS, yet only ~12% of desktop DNS resolutions use DoH (performance.mozilla.org); the rest go through the OS resolver, which may strip them.

  • IPv6 is still a minority of connections (~30%), even at global client scale. Curiously, IPv6 and HTTP/3 have converged: both sit near ~30% and track each other (GLAM: connection_address_type).
  • Real ECH is negotiated on only ~0.3% of TLS handshakes (~1.4% of HTTP/3); almost everything else is GREASE. The ceiling is the ~16% of HTTPS records that carry an ECH config (ssl_handshake_result_ech, http3_ech_outcome).

Transitions can be fast: HTTPS vs IPv6

  • HTTPS went from ~29% of page loads (2013) to 80%+ in about seven years.
  • IPv6 is still only ~42–47% deployed (Google / APNIC) after 25+ years (RFC 2460, 1998).
  • HTTP/3 sits near ~30% today. Which curve will it follow?

  • New connections start slow, and many carry too little data to ever leave slow start.
  • The median connection never even registers a congestion event: 0 at P50 and P95, just 6 at P99.9. Almost nothing ever pushes the path hard enough to see loss or a CE mark.
  • So high-rate send performance (the QUIC-upload debate) touches a small slice of traffic: worth measuring before optimizing.

GLAM: congestion_window_growth, slow_start_exited_filtered, congestion_event_count (release).

ECN works on most paths, and it pays off

  • ~60% of QUIC connections see working ECN end to end; ~35% have marks bleached, only ~3% black-holed.
  • It pays off: on a CE mark Firefox reduces its congestion response without waiting for loss (neqo#3233).
  • ~1 in 5 congestion responses now comes from a CE mark, not packet loss (GLAM: congestion_event_reason).

A real base to build L4S on.

QUIC round-trip times, from the client

Median ~25 ms, but a long tail: P95 ~175 ms, P99.9 ~2 s. The client sees the whole world’s paths, including the slow ones.

Reading server MTUs from the client

The median server datagram is ~1217 B, a conservative QUIC-safe size; only the tail reaches ~1448 B, near the 1500-byte Ethernet MTU. Most servers leave PMTUD headroom on the table. (The tiny sizes are ACKs.)

  • Speculative connects, preconnects, and coalescing open many connections that never carry a request (52% on HTTP/2, 31% on HTTP/3).
  • Two thirds of connections are third-party, and about half go to a classified tracker.

GLAM: spdy_request_per_conn, http3_request_per_conn, http_traffic_analysis, http_request_per_page.

Cutting through the noise

  • Nightly vs release. Nightly catches regressions within hours; release gives representative scale.
  • Roll out behind experiments. Compare arms on the same build, so the effect is the change, not the population.
  • Watch the denominator. Connections, requests, and page loads give different answers to “how much HTTP/3?”
  • The trap. A metric gated on an OS feature is buried by the majority OS. UDP GSO/GRO offload looks absent at os:"*" (Windows dominates), and only appears once you filter os:"Linux".

Takeaways

  • The client side, at global scale, is public: use it.
  • Surprises worth chasing: h3 discovery still wastes a round trip, ECH is barely deployed, most connections never leave slow start, ECN is mostly bleached.
